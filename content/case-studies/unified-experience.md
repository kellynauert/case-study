# MathTrack Learning Platform

## Designing a Unified Learning Experience

### Overview

Traditional learning management systems treat every course as its own destination. Students move between isolated courses, each with its own navigation, organization, and workflow. As both a former Canvas student and the person designing MathTrack's platform, I saw how much unnecessary friction this created.

Rather than recreating that experience, I designed the platform around the student instead of the course. Every feature—from navigation to assignments, grades, and notifications—works together as part of a single application instead of a collection of independent course sites.

The result is a platform that students learn once and use consistently throughout their program.

> **Screenshot:** Student dashboard or Courses page

---

## The Problem

Canvas gives instructors significant freedom to organize their courses. While that flexibility benefits course authors, it often comes at the expense of students.

Every course can have different navigation, different terminology, and different organizational patterns. Students spend time figuring out where information lives instead of focusing on learning.

The problem extends beyond navigation. Assignments, grades, announcements, and notifications are all tied to individual courses. Viewing work across multiple classes requires constantly moving between separate course sites, even though students naturally think about their education as a whole.

I wanted the platform to behave like a single application rather than a collection of independent websites.

---

## Design Principles

Instead of designing around courses, I designed around the student's workflow.

### The platform should have one navigation model

Students should never have to relearn the interface because they started a different course.

### Bring information together

Assignments, grades, notifications, and announcements should feel like parts of the same system rather than separate tools scattered throughout different courses.

### Guide students without restricting them

The platform should reduce unnecessary decisions while still giving students the freedom to navigate however they choose.

---

## Designing the Learning Experience

One of the biggest differences between MathTrack and a traditional LMS is that students rarely have to decide what to do next.

Every student sees a **Continue** card at the top of the application. Whether they land on their dashboard or directly on the Courses page depends on their program, but both experiences begin with the same goal: get students back into learning as quickly as possible.

Instead of asking students to remember which course they were working in, which lesson they had completed, or which assignment was due next, the platform simply takes them back to the next page in their learning path with a single click.

This reduced the amount of navigation required to continue making progress while allowing students to focus on the content rather than the software.

> **Screenshot:** Continue card on the Dashboard or Courses page.

---

The Continue card wasn't designed to replace exploration.

Students can still navigate through their courses however they choose. Every course provides an overview of its structure, allowing students to move between lessons, revisit previous material, or jump ahead when appropriate.

The difference is that navigation becomes intentional instead of required. Students who simply want to continue learning can do so immediately, while students looking for specific content retain full control over how they move through the curriculum.

That balance became an important design principle throughout the platform. The software should guide students without taking control away from them.

> **Screenshot:** Course overview showing lessons and navigation.

---

Another goal was to eliminate the artificial boundaries between courses.

Students don't think about their work one course at a time—they think about everything they need to accomplish. Instead of requiring students to open each course individually to find assignments, grades, or announcements, the platform brings that information together into a single experience.

Whether checking upcoming work, reviewing grades, or catching up on announcements, students interact with one application rather than a collection of disconnected course sites.

> **Screenshot:** Cross-course assignments, grades, or announcements.

---

Consistency became just as important as functionality.

Every course shares the same navigation, page layout, and interaction patterns. Once students learn how to move through one course, they already know how to use every other course on the platform.

That consistency dramatically reduced the amount of time students spent learning the software and made it easier for instructors to focus on teaching rather than explaining where to find things.

> **Screenshot:** Two different courses demonstrating identical navigation and layout.

## Designing for Scale

Designing around a unified student experience made it easier to introduce new features without increasing complexity.

Because every course followed the same structure and interaction model, improvements to navigation, progress tracking, assignments, or notifications immediately benefited every course in the platform. New capabilities became enhancements to a single experience rather than features that had to be implemented differently for every course.

The result was a platform that continued to grow while remaining familiar to the students who used it every day.

> **Screenshot:** Student interface showing multiple unified features working together.

## Outcome

Today, students spend significantly less time navigating the platform and more time engaging with course material.

Before MathTrack, instructors regularly answered questions like:

- Where is my homework?
- Where do I find my grades?
- Which assignment am I supposed to work on next?

Those questions largely disappeared. Instead of helping students navigate the software, instructors were able to focus on teaching and supporting the course content itself.

The platform became intuitive enough that navigation faded into the background—a goal that influenced every design decision throughout the project.

## Reflection

The biggest lesson from this project wasn't about dashboards or navigation—it was about reducing cognitive load.

Students shouldn't have to remember where they left off, relearn the interface every semester, or hunt through multiple courses just to answer simple questions.

By treating the platform as a single, cohesive learning experience instead of a collection of independent course sites, I was able to remove much of that friction and let students focus on what they logged in to do: learn.
