# Designing the Administrative Experience

## Transforming manual operational workflows into self-service software.

### Overview

As MathTrack grew, so did the operational work required to support students. Every new program, enrollment, transcript, and reporting requirement introduced another manual process. Many of those tasks depended on me because there simply wasn't an interface for anyone else to use.

Over time, I designed and built an administrative platform that allowed our team to manage the entire student lifecycle without engineering support. From reviewing applications and enrolling students to generating transcripts and exporting data for state reporting, the platform became the operational backbone of the organization.

The goal wasn't to build another dashboard. It was to give a small team the tools they needed to manage a growing institution independently.

## The Problem

Our administrative staff wear many hats.

On any given day they might be reviewing new applications, enrolling students into courses, moving students between cohorts, answering questions, generating transcripts, or preparing information for state reporting.

Each responsibility had its own workflow, and many required manual intervention because the necessary tools simply didn't exist yet.

Information was spread across multiple systems, with HubSpot handling some administrative tasks while others required direct changes to the application or database. As the platform evolved, internal requests frequently came back to engineering simply because there wasn't a user interface capable of performing the work.

That wasn't sustainable for a growing organization.

---

## Design Principles

Rather than building individual administrative pages, I designed a platform that could evolve alongside the organization.

### Reduce engineering dependencies

Administrative staff should be able to manage the platform without requiring developer assistance for routine tasks.

### Surface information where decisions are made

Important information shouldn't be hidden behind multiple pages or disconnected systems. The interface should provide enough context for administrators to make decisions without constantly switching between screens.

### Design for changing workflows

Administrative responsibilities evolve over time. The platform needed to support new programs, new reporting requirements, and new operational processes without requiring complete redesigns.

---

## Designing the Administrative Experience

User management became the foundation of the platform.

Administrators manage students throughout their entire lifecycle—from reviewing applications and approving admissions to enrolling students in courses, assigning cohorts, maintaining records, and ultimately generating transcripts.

Rather than treating each of those responsibilities as separate systems, I designed them as connected parts of a single workflow centered around the student.

> **Screenshot:** User profile showing enrollment, cohorts, and student information.

---

As the platform expanded, administrative responsibilities expanded with it.

Every new feature introduced another operational task that someone eventually needed to perform. Rather than continuing to expose those capabilities through engineering or direct database changes, I built interfaces that allowed staff to manage the platform themselves.

That shift fundamentally changed how new features were designed. Building the functionality itself was only part of the work. Every feature also needed an administrative experience that made it manageable after launch.

> **Screenshot:** Administrative interface managing platform configuration.

---

Because our team is small, efficiency mattered more than specialization.

The platform wasn't designed for one type of administrator. Student advisors, content authors, graders, and program administrators all interact with the same system from different perspectives.

Rather than building isolated tools for each role, I focused on creating consistent patterns that made it easy to move between responsibilities without relearning the interface.

That consistency reduced training, simplified day-to-day operations, and made it easier for new administrative features to fit naturally into the existing platform.

---

## Designing for Scale

Many administrative workflows began as one-off solutions to immediate problems.

As similar requests appeared over time, I looked for opportunities to generalize them into reusable patterns instead of building entirely new interfaces.

This allowed the platform to grow organically while remaining consistent. New workflows could build on familiar interaction patterns, reducing implementation time while making the system easier for staff to learn and maintain.

The result was an administrative platform that evolved alongside the organization instead of becoming increasingly fragmented as new operational needs emerged.

---

## Outcome

Today, the majority of day-to-day administrative work happens without engineering involvement.

Staff can manage student records, process enrollments, organize cohorts, generate transcripts, and perform many of the operational tasks that previously required developer assistance.

As the organization grew, the platform allowed our small team to support more students and more programs without proportionally increasing administrative overhead.

---

## Reflection

One of the biggest lessons from this project was that internal software deserves the same level of design attention as customer-facing products.

Administrative tools shape how an organization operates every day. By investing in thoughtful workflows instead of treating internal interfaces as an afterthought, I was able to reduce manual work, remove engineering bottlenecks, and give our team the tools they needed to manage the platform independently.

Ultimately, the success of the platform wasn't measured by how many administrative features it contained, but by how rarely someone needed to ask me to make a change on their behalf.
